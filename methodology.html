Methodology — measured, not believed

JustHodl's founding rule: no claim ships without its sample size. Every engine that makes a directional call must log that call to a closed loop where it is graded against real prices out-of-sample, in public, forever. This page explains how the machine measures itself.

…engines on the board

1 · The closed loop (how signals are graded)

When an engine fires, it writes a signal record: prediction, direction, confidence, baseline price, benchmark and check windows (+5/+21/+63 days). A grader revisits each record at those horizons and scores it against what actually happened. A skill aggregator then computes per-engine hit rates, profit factors and rolling 30-day form — visible on /skill.html — and a calibrator automatically shrinks the confidence of any engine whose live record diverges from its claims (n≥20, |error|≥10%). Engines don't get to grade themselves, and they don't get to forget.

2 · Measured, not believed — three examples

The MA "support" folklore. The crowd says the 200-day moving average is where buying liquidity lives. We ran every touch, breakdown and reclaim on 55 years of S&P data (13,952 sessions). Verdict: touch returns are roughly base-rate; the liquidity is real but shows up as shelf depth (median undercut only −0.7% to −1.5% before the bid) — and breaking below the 50DMA in a bull market was historically a buy 70% of the time, not a sell. Death crosses? Only 31% were lower 63 days later. Full tables →
The CISS "dump" signal. Euro-area systemic-stress spikes are sold as crash predictors. Across 26 episodes since 1997, spikes marked capitulation lows, not dump-starts — the real dump risk is the slow percentile build in funding plumbing. The radar says so on its face. EU Dump Radar →
Regime playbooks with n. "Stagflation kills equities" — measured: SPX still returned +2.2% median over the next 3 months across n=161 stagflation months since 1963; it's merely the worst quadrant, and the cleaner tell is credit spreads. Every quadrant claim on /regime.html carries its sample size.

4 · Freshness & honest coverage

Every published brief carries its as-of dates, and a freshness manifest enforces per-feed SLAs — an hourly watchdog alerts on FRESH→STALE transitions. Three honesty rules are enforced in code: (a) staleness-guarded probes — discontinued upstream series (a real, recurring hazard: OECD EA codes, LBMA gold, weekly MMF) are rejected rather than silently served years stale; (b) availability flags — when a pillar can't be measured, composites renormalize and say so instead of pretending; (c) discontinued badges — dead history series are labeled on the page, never hidden. Where a needed feed is paid-only (real MOVE, cross-currency basis, dark pools), we publish the gap and the free proxy we use instead.

5 · What the AI layer is — and isn't

AI briefings on each page are narrators bound to the numbers: each persona is instructed to cite only the engine's measured tables (hit rates, n, medians, event studies) and is forbidden from asserting folklore the data doesn't support. Models summarize; the closed loop decides what was right.
