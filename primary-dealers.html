How to read this desk

Why it matters: dealer inventory is the market's shock absorber — the balance sheet that buys when clients need to sell. A net-short book means that buffer is gone, and it explains why credit tends to crack before equities: there is nobody warehousing the first wave of selling.

Three competing explanations (all can be true at once): 1) risk aversion — spreads near multi-decade tights pay dealers almost nothing to hold duration-heavy credit; 2) demand too hot — the AI-capex issuance wave is absorbed instantly, inventory never builds; 3) structure — post-GFC capital rules plus electronic & portfolio trading (≈49% of IG volume) replaced the warehouse model. The level alone is not a crash signal.

What we actually grade: the maturity twist (long-end short vs short-end long = deliberate rate caution, not coincidence), the rate of de-inventorying (13-week impulse, 52-week z), and the squeeze setup — net short into tight spreads means covering into a market where pensions/insurers rarely sell, which can amplify any rally violently. Cash bonds only; CDS/ETF hedges are invisible here.
